11  The Cox Proportional Hazards Model

The Cox model (1972) is the workhorse of modern survival analysis. It allows the joint effect of multiple covariates on survival to be estimated without specifying the shape of the baseline hazard — a crucial advantage in practice, where the hazard form is rarely known.

11.1 The Model

For a subject with covariate vector \(\mathbf{x} = (x_1,\ldots,x_p)^T\):

\[h(t;\mathbf{x}) = h_0(t)\exp(\beta_1 x_1 + \cdots + \beta_p x_p) = h_0(t)\exp(\boldsymbol{\beta}^T\mathbf{x})\]

  • \(h_0(t)\) is the baseline hazard — the hazard when all covariates are zero
  • \(\exp(\boldsymbol{\beta}^T\mathbf{x})\) is the relative hazard (or risk score)
  • \(\beta_j\) is the log hazard ratio for a one-unit increase in \(x_j\), holding others constant

Key property — Proportional Hazards:

\[\frac{h(t;\mathbf{x})}{h(t;\mathbf{x}^*)} = \exp[\boldsymbol{\beta}^T(\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{x}^*)]\]

The hazard ratio is constant over time — it does not depend on \(t\).

11.2 Cox’s Partial Likelihood

Cox’s insight was that \(\boldsymbol{\beta}\) can be estimated without knowing \(h_0(t)\). At each ordered event time \(t_j\), the probability that subject \((j)\) (the one who failed) is the one who failed, given that exactly one failure occurred in the risk set \(\mathcal{R}(t_j)\):

\[L_j(\boldsymbol{\beta}) = \frac{\exp(\boldsymbol{\beta}^T \mathbf{x}_{(j)})}{\sum_{l \in \mathcal{R}(t_j)} \exp(\boldsymbol{\beta}^T \mathbf{x}_l)}\]

The partial likelihood is \(L_P(\boldsymbol{\beta}) = \prod_{j=1}^k L_j(\boldsymbol{\beta})\).

Maximising \(\log L_P(\boldsymbol{\beta})\) gives \(\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}}\), which is consistent and asymptotically normal.

  select(term, estimate, conf.low, conf.high, p.value) |>

                full_width = FALSE, position = "center") |>
  column_spec(2:4, bold = TRUE)
Interpreting Hazard Ratios
  • HR = 1: no association
  • HR > 1: increased hazard (worse survival) per unit increase in covariate
  • HR < 1: decreased hazard (better survival) — protective factor

Example: sex HR ≈ 0.59 means female patients have a 41% lower risk of death compared to males at any time point (95% CI: 0.42–0.82).

11.5 Testing the Proportional Hazards Assumption

The PH assumption can be tested via Schoenfeld residuals: under PH, the scaled Schoenfeld residuals for covariate \(j\) should be uncorrelated with time.

ph_test <- cox.zph(cox_fit)
print(ph_test)
         chisq df     p
age      0.186  1 0.666
sex      2.059  1 0.151
ph.ecog  1.359  1 0.244

wt.loss  0.110  1 0.740

ggcoxzph(ph_test, ggtheme = theme_bw(base_size = 12))
What to Do When PH Is Violated

If the global test or individual covariate test is significant (\(p < 0.05\)), PH is violated. Options:

  1. Stratify on the violating covariate: coxph(Surv ~ x + strata(z))
  2. Add a time-interaction term: x:log(time)
  3. Use an accelerated failure time (AFT) model (no PH assumption)
  4. Flexible parametric models (e.g., Royston–Parmar spline models)

Historical Insights

Sir David Roxbee Cox (1924–2022) was arguably the most influential statistician of the 20th century. He proposed the proportional hazards model in a landmark 1972 paper in the Journal of the Royal Statistical Society. The paper introduced both the model and the concept of partial likelihood — a method of inference so elegant and novel that it required a completely new theoretical framework to justify. Cox was knighted in 1985 and received virtually every major statistical prize. The Cox model is among the most-cited works in all of science.
